## Topic

The report derives from a paper from Suchy et al. (2024), and intends to give a case example for the following arguments:



<details>

<summary>Negative NPGO (warm-phase) Years of Lower Diatom Biomass</summary>

Based on Figure 5. 'Mean monthly depth‐integrated (0–100 m) diatom and nanoflagellate biomass averaged over (a) cold and (b) warm years in the Central Strait of Georgia, BC. Error bars represent the standard deviation of the mean.'

From section 3.6, 'Warm‐phase years exhibited lower overall summer diatom biomass (t‐test, t(3) = 2.82, p = 0.03) and an earlier shift to nanoflagellate dominance compared to cold‐phase years.' 

A case of 2011 (Positive NPGO) and 2018 (Negative NPGO) could provide evidence (not prove) the argument that negative NPGO comes with lower Diatom Biomass, or lower Diatom/Flagellate ratio. 

</details>




<details>

<summary>Wind causes nutrient supply</summary>

From Section 3.7: 'Previously, Moore‐Maley and Allen (2022) determined that wind‐driven upwelling during the summer months results in periodic nutrient resupply to the surface waters in the SoG.'  

PCA/EOF shows winds could result in surface salinity response, which indicates a nutrient resupply. 

</details>



<details>

<summary>Wind causes diatom bloom in Northern SoG</summary>

From Section 3.7: 'In addition, wind events in the Northern SoG have been shown to result in episodic diatom blooms during summer (Del Bel Belluz et al., 2021).'  

EOF/PCA could support that wind events cause nitrate resupply. Biomass change at different points during storm could show that strong winds could cause diatom blooms (hopefully). 

</details>


<details>

<summary>Upwelling and nitrate supply and diatom</summary>

'These upwelling events are critical for resupplying nitrate to the surface waters and tend to favor the opportunist‐type diatoms in the model over the slower‐growing nanoflagellates which tend to do better in low nitrate conditions (Jarníková et al., 2022).'

PCA/EOF could support that upwelling events cause nitrite resupplying and phytoplankton biomass change during storm could support the argument that diatom benefits more from it. 

</details>

'The Strait of Georgia is by far the most important marine region of British Columbia.'

'On the average, it is about 222 km (120 nm) long and 28 km (15 nm) wide; islands occupy roughly 7% of its total surface area of 6800 km2 (200 nm2).'
